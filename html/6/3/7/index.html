Town Walls

  • Tour information

    Swansea’s main market place was held at the top of Wind Street. Food, clothes and household items would be traded here. Archaeological finds show that fine imported wine was drunk in Swansea in the Middle Ages. Trade in leather and hides was also an important part of the local economy. At the bottom of Wind Street, the South Gate led out to marshland and a ferry across the Tawe. At the top of the street, the Castle loomed large.

    About this place

    For medieval Swansea, like all medieval towns, the market place was its commercial core, where traders gathered. The market place in Wind Street is probably the earliest part of medieval Swansea, perhaps laid out at the same time the castle was first built, in around 1100. Its location, beneath the castle, meant it could be watched closely by the Lords of the Gower. By 1300 it was perhaps home to some of its wealthiest residents. Swansea had a weekly market and two yearly fairs, and on other days the market place was used for meetings and street entertainment during religious festivals. Food was one of the main goods sold, as well as clothes and household items. These were sold from stalls and booths which could be set up and taken down on market days.

    Connection with William Cragh

    Adam of Loughor was a 13-year-old boy in 1290, when the Welsh rebel William Cragh was hanged in Swansea. Interviewed by representatives of the pope years later, Adam remembers that he sat on Swansea’s town walls to get a good view of the hanging. Adam’s story reminds us that executions served as popular spectacle in the Middle Ages – a tense and complex mix of entertainment and reminder of the lord’s power and vengeance. Although Adam sought out the walls as a good vantage-point to watch the hanging, he wasn’t so happy about seeing Cragh’s dead body close up. He tells us that, later, he was too scared to look at Cragh’s bloodied corpse when it lay in the house of Thomas Mathews.

    Medieval Object

    • Medieval cannon ball, found at the site of the Cross Keys Inn (courtesy of Swansea Museum)

    Medieval Swansea was often under siege. This cannon ball reminds us of the many attacks which the town walls had to withstand, and their important role in protecting the town’s inhabitants. Tradition holds that this particular cannon ball was used later, in Oliver Cromwell’s attack on Swansea Castle in the Civil War (1648).
