High Street

  • Tour information

    Like many medieval towns, Swansea was defended with walls to protect it against attacks. The walls of medieval Swansea ran along the west and north of the town – the east was already bounded by the River Tawe and to the south lay marshland and the Cadle stream. Here, the medieval walls ran along the line of Whitewalls Street. Archaeological excavations were under the red-brick shop on the corner (currently Primark). Just south of here, the Wassail Gate opened out onto the road to Gower. To the north, the town’s West Gate led out to the Washing Pool where the townsfolk may have come to do their laundry.

    About this place

    Medieval Swansea was a walled town. The line of walls is in part recalled by the name Whitewalls and ran on the western (inland) side of the town connecting West Gate and Wassail Gate. The walls here were made of stone. Historical documents refer to a tax in 1317 for the upkeep of the walls while archaeology has shown that the stone defences may be contemporary with the rebuilding of the castle in the late 13th century. At the corner of Whitewalls and St Mary’s Square, the wall was shown to be 1.9 metres thick, faced with sandstone blocks. In front of the wall was a ditch about 6 metres wide and 2 metres deep. The line of the defences around the town can be followed by walking along the street still called Whitewalls.

    Connection with William Cragh

    Running north through the town, the High Street was the main route between Swansea’s New Castle and the probable location of the house of Thomas Mathews, near the Chapel of St John. It was in the house of Thomas Mathews that William Cragh’s body was laid after the hanging – and where he gradually revived. Lord William de Briouze’s steward, John of Baggeham, made the journey along the High Street repeatedly as he hurried between the Castle and Thomas Mathews’ house to observe and report on the situation. Lady Mary’s maids also went back and forth along the High Street with broth and almond milk for the recovering Cragh.

    Medieval Object

    • Rotary quern base and runner stone for milling, from Knelston, Gower (courtesy of Swansea Museum)

    These stones would have been used for grinding corn to mill flour, an important commodity for trade and consumption in medieval Swansea. A Swansea charter in the 12th century gives all burgesses the right to have their own oven for baking bread, although they were not allowed to grind corn in their own houses. This meant that they had to use the lord’s mill and pay for the privilege!
