The Dungeon

  • Tour information

    The North Gate was one of the points where entry into medieval Swansea was controlled. Swords, daggers and cannon balls found in Swansea remind us of its violent past. Many people met a bloody end here in the Middle Ages. Medieval Welsh poetry calls Swansea a ‘town of broken towers’ where the women are ‘altogether widows’ as a result of Welsh raids.

    About this place

    North Gate stood on High Street guarding the main entrance into the town from the north. The gate no longer stands but the line of the town walls here is preserved in the alignment of Morris Lane and King Street. Archaeologists working here discovered parts of the medieval town-ditch which showed it to be 10m wide and 3 metres deep. North Gate controlled access into and out of the town, and divided those inside the walled town from those outside, in the suburbs beyond the wall, known in historical documents as Above Town.

    Connection with William Cragh

    William Cragh was held in the dungeons of Swansea’s New Castle before he was led to Gibbet Hill to be hanged. While in the dungeons, he tells us that he had a vision of a man robed in white, whom he believed to be St Thomas de Cantilupe. He also saw a vision of the Virgin Mary, who gave him a sign that he would be saved. While in the dungeon, Cragh ‘bent a penny to St Thomas’ – a way of asking for the saint’s help and promising to honour him if he was spared.

    Medieval Object

    • Silver coin from the reign of Edward I (1272-1307), found in Upper Killay (courtesy of Swansea Museum)

    Waiting in the dungeons, the night before his execution, William Cragh tells us that he ‘bent a penny’ to St Thomas. This folk custom involved bending or folding a coin to invoke a saint and their help. This coin, from the reign of Edward I, recalls the secret ritual practised by Cragh – which may perhaps have saved his life…
