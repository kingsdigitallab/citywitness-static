St David's Hospital

  • Tour information

    Finding the Chapel of St John closed, Cragh’s friends carried his body to the house of a local townsman, Thomas Mathews. Although we don’t know the exact location of Thomas Mathews’ house, it was close to St John’s, and this area of the High Street still shows the pattern of medieval plots fronting the street. While lying in this house, William Cragh gradually came back to life. Was it a miracle?

    About this place

    Swansea’s townsfolk lived in houses fronting onto the town’s streets, but what these houses looked like is hard to say. Very few medieval buildings have been excavated in Swansea, but some on Goat Street have. The houses here were timber framed with tiled roofs and decorated ridge tiles. In the William Cragh story, Thomas Matthews’ house is mentioned, standing close to St John’s church. Exactly where his house stood is impossible to say, but interestingly along this part of High Street there are still surviving the patterns of medieval house-plots of the kind that would have been there in Cragh’s day. The front of the house-plot in medieval Swansea might be used for shops or workshops, with living accommodation at the rear or on the upper floors. To the rear was a long plot which would have formed a yard, with out-buildings used for storage and perhaps industrial activity, with pits used for toilets and the disposal of rubbish. Here too was space for a vegetable plot and somewhere to keep animals.

    Connection with William Cragh

    The Hospital of St David was founded after the hanging of William Cragh, in 1332 (Cragh was hanged in 1290). But the values of the Hospital reflect medieval traditions of charity, alms-giving and care for the sick and poor which we also glimpse in the story of William Cragh. Lady Mary de Briouze, wife of the Lord William who orders Cragh’s execution, performs acts of charity often associated with medieval noblewomen by sending almond milk and broth to help nourish Cragh after the hanging. There’s a fascinating tension here between Mary’s duty to support her husband’s rule as Marcher lord, and her Christian obligation to support the vulnerable members of Swansea’s community.

    Medieval Object

    • Earthenware flagon, found at the Cross Keys Inn excavation (courtesy of Swansea Museum)

    Found on this exact site, this earthenware flagon reflects the comfortable lifestyle of the inmates of St David’s Hospital in the Middle Ages. Like others found at the site, this flagon may have held fine wine imported from France – obviously of medicinal benefit for the hospital’s sick and elderly residents!
