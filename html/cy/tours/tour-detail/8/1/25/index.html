Rhiw’r Crocbren

  • Tour information

    Wind Street curves around in an arc and is broad to accommodate medieval Swansea’s market. The street may represent the earliest phase of urban development. On east side of the street house plots stretched back to the river and on the west to a ditch, possibly the town’s earliest medieval defences linked with the castle. One interpretation of Wind Street and the market is to see it as a late-11th or early-12th century piece of town planning linked to the creation of Old Castle to the north. The castle and town together were a hallmark of Norman colonisation and urban development in Wales and England. A second interpretation of Wind Street is to see it as an area that existed before the Normans. If so, then the possibility exists that the curved Wind Street was originally longer and perhaps even originated at the time of the Vikings.

    Yn Abertawe'r Oesoedd Canol, fel y rhan fwyaf o drefi canoloesol, y farchnad oedd y canolbwynt masnachol lle byddai masnachwyr yn ymgasglu. Y farchnad yn Stryd y Gwynt oedd y rhan gynharaf o Abertawe'r Oesoedd Canol mwyaf tebyg ac mae'n bosib y cafodd ei sefydlu pan adeiladwyd y castell am y tro cyntaf, tua 1100. Roedd ei lleoliad, islaw'r castell, yn golygu y gallai Arglwyddiaeth Gŵyr gadw llygad barcut arni. Erbyn 1300 mae'n bosib mai dyma lle'r oedd rhai o drigolion cyfoethocaf Abertawe'n byw. Roedd gan Abertawe farchnad wythnosol a dwy ffair y flwyddyn, ac ar ddyddiau eraill byddai'r ardal yn cael ei defnyddio i gynnal cyfarfodydd ac adloniant stryd yn ystod gwyliau crefyddol. Bwyd oedd un o'r prif nwyddau a werthwyd, yn ogystal â dillad a nwyddau cartref. Byddent yn cael eu gwerthu o stondinau a bythau a gâi eu gosod yn arbennig ar gyfer dyddiau'r farchnad.

    Connection with William Cragh

    On an autumn day in the year 1290, William Cragh was hanged on Gibbet Hill by William de Briouze, the Anglo-Norman lord of Gower. Cragh was hanged as a rebel and criminal, together with another Welshman, Trahaearn ap Hywel. In an act of particular cruelty, Lord William forced Cragh’s own friends and family to perform the hanging. Cragh was lifted onto the gibbet first, and then Trahaearn was pulled up by a rope slung over the crossbeam. But one medieval witness at the execution tells us that Trahaearn was ‘big and heavy’, and this caused the gibbet to break. Cragh and Trahaearn were hanged for a second time and seemed to be dead. But was that really the case?

    Medieval Object

    • Floor tile depicting Norman coat of arms, from Neath Abbey (courtesy of Swansea Museum)

       

    With its depiction of the Norman coat of arms of England (three lions passant), this tile represents the power of Anglo-Norman rulers in Wales in the Middle Ages. The lion emblems were introduced to England after the Norman Conquest, and this coat of arms was first used by King Richard I.
