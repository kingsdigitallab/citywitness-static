Y Ddaeargell

  • Tour information

    Wind Street curves around in an arc and is broad to accommodate medieval Swansea’s market. The street may represent the earliest phase of urban development. On east side of the street house plots stretched back to the river and on the west to a ditch, possibly the town’s earliest medieval defences linked with the castle. One interpretation of Wind Street and the market is to see it as a late-11th or early-12th century piece of town planning linked to the creation of Old Castle to the north. The castle and town together were a hallmark of Norman colonisation and urban development in Wales and England. A second interpretation of Wind Street is to see it as an area that existed before the Normans. If so, then the possibility exists that the curved Wind Street was originally longer and perhaps even originated at the time of the Vikings.

    Connection with William Cragh

    William Cragh was held in the dungeons of Swansea’s New Castle before he was led to Gibbet Hill to be hanged. While in the dungeons, he tells us that he had a vision of a man robed in white, whom he believed to be St Thomas de Cantilupe. He also saw a vision of the Virgin Mary, who gave him a sign that he would be saved. While in the dungeon, Cragh ‘bent a penny to St Thomas’ – a way of asking for the saint’s help and promising to honour him if he was spared.

    • Silver coin from the reign of Edward I (1272-1307), found in Upper Killay (courtesy of Swansea Museum)

    Waiting in the dungeons, the night before his execution, William Cragh tells us that he ‘bent a penny’ to St Thomas. This folk custom involved bending or folding a coin to invoke a saint and their help. This coin, from the reign of Edward I, recalls the secret ritual practised by Cragh – which may perhaps have saved his life…
