Y Strand

  • Tour information

    Old Castle is no longer visible but was the site of Swansea’s first castle, built in around 1100. The castle itself was a motte and bailey, covering quite a large site and connected on one side to the market place and Wind Street. The castle was enclosed on all four sides by natural defences, but these were also strengthened by walls and gates. It is possible that the outer part of the castle enclosure, the bailey, was used to control traffic coming into and out of the market place, passing before the lord’s eyes. If there was a town at Swansea before the Normans, their castle may have been built upon house-plots and streets, as happened elsewhere at this time, at Norwich for example. By-passing the castle was made possible by using Goat Street, which skirts to the west of the castle, or Strand to the east, avoiding the lord’s prying eyes.

    About this place

    Yr Hen Gastell oedd lleoliad castell cyntaf Abertawe, a sefydlwyd tua 1106. Roedd yn gastell mwnt a beili, sydd bellach wedi diflannu o'r treflun modern. Pan gafodd ei adeiladu, byddai'r castell wedi bod yn symbol o bŵer Normanaidd yn nhirlun Cymru. Rhannwyd y castell yn ddwy ran, sef wardiau neu feilïau, gyda ffos a phalis o goed ar gyfer yr amddiffynfeydd a dau borth. Roedd un porth ar ochr ogleddol y castell, yn arwain at y Stryd Fawr, a'r llall ar yr ochr ddeheuol, gyferbyn â'r Castell Newydd. Roedd ward allanol yr Hen Gastell yn cynnwys nifer o'r adeiladau yr oedd eu hangen i'w gynnal a'i gadw. Y ward fewnol oedd calon amddiffynnol y castell gyda'i wal ei hun a ffos o amgylch twmpath (mwnt) pridd a thŵr ar ei ben. Ym 1216 roedd y castell dan reolaeth y teulu de Braose, ac ym 1221 atgyweiriodd John de Braose y castell a'i ailadeiladu mewn carreg. Ym 1845, darganfuwyd gweddillion hen borth y Stryd Fawr o'r 13eg ganrif a datgelwyd adeilad trawiadol gyda dau dŵr bob ochr i'r fynedfa. Mewn blynyddoedd diweddarach mae archaeolegwyr hefyd wedi dod o hyd i rannau o ffos y castell ger Stryd y Coleg a Ffordd y Dywysoges.

    Connection with William Cragh

    In the late 13th century, the Strand ran along the River Tawe, where ships unloaded their goods. Swansea’s status as a port and trading city is reflected in Welsh poetry, which celebrates the town as ‘fair mother of ships’. But the strand, and the land around it which sloped down to the river, was overlooked by Swansea Castle and the Anglo-Norman lords who controlled the town. The story of the hanging of William Cragh in 1290 shows us how the ruling de Briouze family were able to look out over the surrounding landscape from the castle, helping them to enforce their power and authority.

    Medieval Object

    • Early medieval pot, from a shipwreck (courtesy of Swansea Museum)

    In the Middle Ages, the Strand ran along the River Tawe and was a bustling place where ships unloaded their wares and moored for repairs. Not all ships made it safely to harbour: this early medieval pot was brought up from a shipwreck by fishing nets in the 19th century. It reflects medieval Swansea’s importance as a trading centre and busy sea-port.
