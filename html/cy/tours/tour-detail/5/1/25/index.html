    Daeargell - Ym 1290, carcharwyd y Cymro William Cragh gan William de Braose, Arglwydd Eingl-Normanaidd Gŵyr, am iddo gymryd rhan mewn gwrthryfel ac ymosod ar Gastell Ystumllwynarth, y Mwmbwls. Y noson cyn ei ddienyddiad roedd Cragh yn cael ei ddal yma, yn naeargelloedd Castell Abertawe, ble cafodd weledigaethau o'r Forwyn Fair a San Thomas de Cantilupe, sant poblogaidd.

    About this place

    Mae cyfeiriadau i ddaeargelloedd y castell ym 1322, 1400 a 1535 pan gawsant gloeon ac allweddi newydd. Ond er gwaethaf hyn, nid ydym yn gwybod lle'r oedd y ddaeargell yng Nghastell Abertawe. Sefydlwyd carchar diweddarach ar gyfer dyledwyr yn nhŵr sgwâr y wal amddiffynnol a oedd o amgylch y castell. Yn yr Oesoedd Canol, byddai gan y tŵr hwn olygfeydd o'r Strand ac afon Tawe gyda'r caeau agored a'r bryniau ar yr ochr arall. Yn y 18fed ganrif addaswyd llawr uchaf y tŵr yn garchar dyledwyr, gan ailsefydlu'r hen gysylltiad rhwng y Castell â chosbi carcharwyr a oedd yn rhan o wrthryfeloedd, megis ymosodiad William Cragh ar Gastell Abertawe a Chastell Ystumllwynarth ym 1287.

    Connection with William Cragh

    On an autumn day in the year 1290, William Cragh was hanged on Gibbet Hill by William de Briouze, the Anglo-Norman lord of Gower. Cragh was hanged as a rebel and criminal, together with another Welshman, Trahaearn ap Hywel. In an act of particular cruelty, Lord William forced Cragh’s own friends and family to perform the hanging. Cragh was lifted onto the gibbet first, and then Trahaearn was pulled up by a rope slung over the crossbeam. But one medieval witness at the execution tells us that Trahaearn was ‘big and heavy’, and this caused the gibbet to break. Cragh and Trahaearn were hanged for a second time and seemed to be dead. But was that really the case?

    Medieval Object

    • Floor tile depicting Norman coat of arms, from Neath Abbey (courtesy of Swansea Museum)

       

    With its depiction of the Norman coat of arms of England (three lions passant), this tile represents the power of Anglo-Norman rulers in Wales in the Middle Ages. The lion emblems were introduced to England after the Norman Conquest, and this coat of arms was first used by King Richard I.
