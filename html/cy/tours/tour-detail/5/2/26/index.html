    Bryn y Crocbren (nodwr â golygfa tua'r bryn) - O'r man hwn cewch olygfa i fyny North Hill, a adnabuwyd fel Bryn y Crocbren yn yr Oesoedd Canol, lle crogwyd William Cragh ar fore hydrefol ym 1290. Ond, aeth y dienyddiad o'i le: torrodd drawst y crocbren a disgynnodd Cragh i'r llawr. Fe'i llusgwyd yn ôl i fyny a'i grogi am yr eildro.

    About this place

    Bryn y Crocbren oedd man dienyddio Abertawe. North Hill yw ei enw bellach, ond tan y 19eg ganrif câi ei adnabod fel Bryn y Crocbren, gyda Heol y Crocbren, a elwir yn Heol North Hill heddiw, yn arwain o'r bryn i'r dref. Roedd lleoliad fel hwn ar gyrion y dref yn fan cyffredin ar gyfer dienyddio a barnu yn yr Oesoedd Canol, ac roedd yn gwneud y crogi'n sioe gyhoeddus gan y byddai'r carcharwyr yn cael eu harwain o'r carchar yn y castell, trwy'r strydoedd i Fryn y Crocbren.

    Connection with William Cragh

    Here, in what is probably the oldest part of Swansea, was the town’s main market place. The site was close to the castle and the power of the Anglo-Norman lords. Food, clothes and other goods would have been traded here. One of the witnesses to the hanging of William Cragh has the name Henry Skinner, which suggests that he may have been involved in the local leather trade. It’s possible that other places were also used for trading and occasional markets. The Welshman John ap Hywel tells us that he watched the hanging of William Cragh from a ‘platea’ (an open space or square) by St Mary’s Church. This may have been the open area just outside the Wassail Gate, near St Mary’s, which has many features typical of market places in medieval towns. This may have been an alternative market place used by the local Welsh community.

    Medieval Object

    • Silver coin from the reign of Edward II (1307-27), found at Upper Killay (courtesy of Swansea Museum)

    This coin may well have changed hands in Swansea’s marketplace, located at the upper end of Wind Street. Goods of all kinds were sold here, and the trade in hides and leather goods was particularly important in medieval Swansea. A late 12th-century charter granted to the burgesses by William de Beaumont included a clause banning foreign merchants from selling cloth in Swansea or buying skins from anyone other than citizens of the town. This was a way of protecting and supporting the local economy.
