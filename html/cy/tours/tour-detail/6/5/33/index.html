Yn y 19eg ganrif, bu'r awdur lleol Samuel Gamwell - hanesydd a chadwraethwr brwd - yn cwyno am arddull bensaernïol ddryslyd yr eglwys.  Mewn pennill, ysgrifennodd:

    The Vicar he vowed, as he peeped round his pulpit:
    ‘Throughout the broad land you may carefully search,
    But you nowhere will find so consummately ugly,
    So rickety, ramshackle, wretched a Church,
    As this pitiful patchwork, St Mary’s of Swansea…’

    About this place

    Eglwys y Santes Fair oedd eglwys blwyf Abertawe'r Oesoedd Canol. Mae'r cofnod cyntaf ohoni ym 1291, ond mae'n debyg y cafodd ei hadeiladu yn y 12fed ganrif pan ddatblygwyd y rhan hon o'r dref gyntaf. Mae'r adeilad wedi newid llawer dros y blynyddoedd, sy'n golygu mai ychydig yn unig o wybodaeth sydd gennym am sut yr edrychai yn yr Oesoedd Canol. Cafodd yr eglwys ei hailadeiladu ym 1739, ond cadwyd tŵr a chapel yr hen eglwys. Cofnodwyd rhai elfennau o'r eglwys ganoloesol sy'n dangos fod ganddi nodweddion pensaernïol o'r 13eg ganrif cyn y cafodd ei hailadeiladu rhwng 1895 a 1899. Cafodd Eglwys y Santes Fair ei difrodi'n ddifrifol yn yr Ail Ryfel Byd, gan ddinistrio'r elfennau canoloesol a oedd ar ôl. Adeiladwyd yr eglwys newydd rhwng 1954-59, gydag ond delw garreg o offeiriad a nodweddion pres beddrod Syr Hugh Johnys a'i wraig o'r 15fed ganrif i'n hatgoffa o'i gorffennol canoloesol.

    Connection with William Cragh

    Running north through the town, the High Street was the main route between Swansea’s New Castle and the probable location of the house of Thomas Mathews, near the Chapel of St John. It was in the house of Thomas Mathews that William Cragh’s body was laid after the hanging – and where he gradually revived. Lord William de Briouze’s steward, John of Baggeham, made the journey along the High Street repeatedly as he hurried between the Castle and Thomas Mathews’ house to observe and report on the situation. Lady Mary’s maids also went back and forth along the High Street with broth and almond milk for the recovering Cragh.

    Medieval Object

    • Rotary quern base and runner stone for milling, from Knelston, Gower (courtesy of Swansea Museum)

    These stones would have been used for grinding corn to mill flour, an important commodity for trade and consumption in medieval Swansea. A Swansea charter in the 12th century gives all burgesses the right to have their own oven for baking bread, although they were not allowed to grind corn in their own houses. This meant that they had to use the lord’s mill and pay for the privilege!
