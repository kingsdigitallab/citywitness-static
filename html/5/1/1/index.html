St John's Chapel

  • Tour information

    In 1290, the Welshman William Cragh was captured by William de Briouze, the Anglo-Norman Lord of Gower, for his part in a rebellion and attack on Oystermouth Castle, in Mumbles. The night before his execution, Cragh was held here in the dungeons of Swansea Castle, where he received visions of the Virgin Mary and St Thomas de Cantilupe, a popular saint.

    About this place

    There are references to the dungeon at the castle in 1322, 1400 and 1535 when it was provided with new locks and keys. Despite this where the dungeon was located in Swansea Castle is not known. A later prison for debtors was established in the square tower set in the defensive wall surrounding the castle. In the medieval period, the view from this tower would have looked over the Strand and the River Tawe to the open fields and hills on the opposite bank. In the 18th century the upper floor of the tower was modified to serve as the debtors’ prison, re-establishing the centuries-old link between the Castle and the detention of prisoners involved in rebellions such as William Cragh’s attack on Swansea and Oystermouth Castles in 1287.

    Connection with William Cragh

    After William Cragh was hanged, his friends carried his apparently lifeless body to the Chapel of St John. This may have been because the Chapel had a long-standing association with the Welsh community, and it was located outside the town walls. Lady Mary de Briouze tells us that Cragh’s body was transported on a kind of wooden wheel, which in itself would have caused great injury. The different medieval witnesses don’t agree on what happened when Cragh’s body arrived at the Chapel of St John. It seems likely that the Chapel was closed, and Cragh’s body was then carried to the nearby house of Thomas Mathews.

    Medieval Object

    • 13th-century incense burner, found at Penmaen Church (courtesy of Swansea Museum)

    Incense was burned in medieval churches to release perfumed smoke during services. This incense burner or censer is in shaped like an ornate tower, representing the city of God – the heavenly Jerusalem. It reminds us that the heavenly Jerusalem was seen as a model for the earthly city in the Middle Ages – an ideal of perfect peace and harmony, ruled by God, with every member of the community in their proper place.
