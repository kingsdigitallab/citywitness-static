High Street

  • Tour information

    In 1290, the Welshman William Cragh was captured by William de Briouze, the Anglo-Norman Lord of Gower, for his part in a rebellion and attack on Oystermouth Castle, in Mumbles. The night before his execution, Cragh was held here in the dungeons of Swansea Castle, where he received visions of the Virgin Mary and St Thomas de Cantilupe, a popular saint.

    About this place

    There are references to the dungeon at the castle in 1322, 1400 and 1535 when it was provided with new locks and keys. Despite this where the dungeon was located in Swansea Castle is not known. A later prison for debtors was established in the square tower set in the defensive wall surrounding the castle. In the medieval period, the view from this tower would have looked over the Strand and the River Tawe to the open fields and hills on the opposite bank. In the 18th century the upper floor of the tower was modified to serve as the debtors’ prison, re-establishing the centuries-old link between the Castle and the detention of prisoners involved in rebellions such as William Cragh’s attack on Swansea and Oystermouth Castles in 1287.

    Connection with William Cragh

    Running north through the town, the High Street was the main route between Swansea’s New Castle and the probable location of the house of Thomas Mathews, near the Chapel of St John. It was in the house of Thomas Mathews that William Cragh’s body was laid after the hanging – and where he gradually revived. Lord William de Briouze’s steward, John of Baggeham, made the journey along the High Street repeatedly as he hurried between the Castle and Thomas Mathews’ house to observe and report on the situation. Lady Mary’s maids also went back and forth along the High Street with broth and almond milk for the recovering Cragh.

    Medieval Object

    • Rotary quern base and runner stone for milling, from Knelston, Gower (courtesy of Swansea Museum)

    These stones would have been used for grinding corn to mill flour, an important commodity for trade and consumption in medieval Swansea. A Swansea charter in the 12th century gives all burgesses the right to have their own oven for baking bread, although they were not allowed to grind corn in their own houses. This meant that they had to use the lord’s mill and pay for the privilege!
