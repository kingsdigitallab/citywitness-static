Adeiladwyd yr eglwys gyntaf yn y 12fed ganrif mwyaf tebyg, a chafodd ei hailfodelu ym 1739 (er y cadwyd tŵr a chapel yr hen eglwys). Yn y 19eg ganrif, bu'r awdur lleol Samuel Gamwell - hanesydd a chadwraethwr brwd - yn cwyno am arddull bensaernïol ddryslyd yr eglwys.  Mewn pennill, ysgrifennodd:

    The Vicar he vowed, as he peeped round his pulpit:
    ‘Throughout the broad land you may carefully search,
    But you nowhere will find so consummately ugly,
    So rickety, ramshackle, wretched a Church,
    As this pitiful patchwork, St Mary’s of Swansea…’

    Fodd bynnag, bu bron i Eglwys y Santes Fair gael ei dinistrio'n gyfan gwbl ym Mlitz Abertawe ac mae'r adeilad presennol yn dyddio'n ôl i'r 1950au.

    About this place

    Eglwys y Santes Fair oedd eglwys blwyf Abertawe'r Oesoedd Canol. Mae'r cofnod cyntaf ohoni ym 1291, ond mae'n debyg y cafodd ei hadeiladu yn y 12fed ganrif pan ddatblygwyd y rhan hon o'r dref gyntaf. Mae'r adeilad wedi newid llawer dros y blynyddoedd, sy'n golygu mai ychydig yn unig o wybodaeth sydd gennym am sut yr edrychai yn yr Oesoedd Canol. Cafodd yr eglwys ei hailadeiladu ym 1739, ond cadwyd tŵr a chapel yr hen eglwys. Cofnodwyd rhai elfennau o'r eglwys ganoloesol sy'n dangos fod ganddi nodweddion pensaernïol o'r 13eg ganrif cyn y cafodd ei hailadeiladu rhwng 1895 a 1899. Cafodd Eglwys y Santes Fair ei difrodi'n ddifrifol yn yr Ail Ryfel Byd, gan ddinistrio'r elfennau canoloesol a oedd ar ôl. Adeiladwyd yr eglwys newydd rhwng 1954-59, gydag ond delw garreg o offeiriad a nodweddion pres beddrod Syr Hugh Johnys a'i wraig o'r 15fed ganrif i'n hatgoffa o'i gorffennol canoloesol.

    Connection with William Cragh

    The parish church for medieval Swansea, St Mary’s benefited from the patronage of the de Briouze family. We know that Lady Mary de Briouze – wife of the Lord William who ordered the hanging of the Welshman William Cragh – was a religious and devout woman, and we can imagine her visiting St Mary’s for services and prayer. While the medieval structure of St Mary’s doesn’t survive today, the story of William Cragh also gives us other clues about what might have been there. For example, while Cragh is in the dungeons of Swansea Castle before his hanging, he reports seeing a vision of the Virgin Mary. But he says that she wasn’t holding her son, the Christ child, ‘as material images concerning her represent’. It’s possible that Cragh was thinking of pictures of Mary which he had seen in Swansea’s parish church, itself dedicated to her. Wall paintings and statues of Mary were very common in Wales at this time.

    Medieval Object

    • Carved stone head, possibly from the medieval St Mary’s Church (courtesy of Swansea Museum)

    This carved stone head was found at the site of the old Rectory House in Fisher Street, Swansea in the late 19th century and is thought to come from the medieval St Mary’s Church. It is traditionally believed to depict Alina de Mowbray, daughter of Lord William de Briouze (junior) who testified in 1307 as a witness to the hanging of William Cragh. Alina’s own life was colourful and her fortunes very changeable. Today, she is remembered as the ghostly ‘White Lady’ of Oystermouth Castle in Mumbles.
