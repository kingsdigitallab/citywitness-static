    About this place

    Roedd trigolion Abertawe'n byw mewn tai a oedd yn wynebu strydoedd y dref, ond mae'n anodd dweud sut roedd y tai hyn yn edrych. Ychydig iawn o adeiladau canoloesol sydd wedi cael eu cloddio'n Abertawe, ond darganfuwyd rhai ar Stryd y Geifr. Roedd gan y tai hyn fframiau coed gyda thoeon teils a theils crib wedi'u haddurno. Yn stori William Cragh, sonnir bod t天 Thomas Matthews ger Eglwys Sant Ioan. Mae'n amhosib pennu union le ei gartref, ond mae'n ddiddorol gweld bod olion lleiniau'r Oesoedd Canol wedi goroesi yn y rhan hon o'r Stryd Fawr; tebyg i'r rhai a fyddai o gwmpas yng nghyfnod Cragh. Mae'n bosib y defnyddiwyd tu blaen y tai yn Abertawe'r Oesoedd Canol fel siopau neu weithdai, gyda llety preswyl yn y cefn neu ar y lloriau uwch. Yn y cefn byddai llain hir a fyddai'n rhan o fuarth, gydag adeiladau allanol i storio neu i wneud gwaith diwydiannol, a thyllau a ddefnyddiwyd fel t天 bach ac i waredu sbwriel. Hefyd byddai lle yma i dyfu llysiau ac i gadw anifeiliaid.

    Connection with William Cragh

    After he was hanged, the body of William Cragh was carried to the house of Thomas Mathews, a burgess (townsman) of Swansea. His body lay here, bloody and disfigured, with his eyes hanging from their sockets and the tongue swollen out of his mouth. Adam of Loughor, aged around 13 at the time, tells us he was too scared to look at the body. And yet, to everyone’s amazement, William Cragh began to come back to life. Many witnesses came to see this and Lady Mary de Briouze – wife of Lord William who had ordered the execution – sent a maidservant with nourishing almond milk to help Cragh recover. Was it a miracle?

    Medieval Object

    • Bronze button, found in Penmaen Burrows (courtesy of Swansea Museum)

    We don’t know the exact location of the house of Thomas Mathews in medieval Swansea – and we don’t know who once wore this tiny bronze button. The button could have slipped from the clothing of any reasonably well-to-do citizen of medieval Swansea: it’s something quite ordinary and quite small, but which suggests an intimate and direct connection with an unknown man or woman in the medieval past of Swansea and the Gower.
