  • Tour information

    Standing here in the Middle Ages you would have been at the edge of the River Tawe and in the midst of a bustling harbour. But not every ship made it safely into port. The object you can view for this location is an early medieval pot, brought up from a shipwreck by fishing nets in the 19th century. Many boats and seafarers have been lost in the waters around Swansea. Traces of some of these lost souls may well be under your feet right here, deep beneath the earth, where the river once flowed.

    About this place

    Roedd masnach forwrol yn rhan allweddol o hanes llewyrchus Abertawe fel tref a phorthladd. Byddai llongau yn angori yn y Strand, ac ystyr yr enw yw traeth neu lan. Mae'r ffordd fodern a elwir Y Strand yn nodi llinell ganoloesol glannau'r Tawe lle byddai llongau'n cael eu tynnu o'r afon i'w dadlwytho neu eu trwsio. Byddai nwyddau a gyrhaeddai Abertawe dros y môr yn cael eu cludo trwy'r strydoedd a oedd yn arwain i fyny o'r Strand. Mae sawl lôn gul wedi goroesi, megis Lôn y Ddraig Werdd, a oedd yn cysylltu'r Strand â phrif farchnad ganoloesol y dref ar Stryd y Gwynt. Mae dogfennau hanesyddol a thystiolaeth archaeolegol yn dangos fod gan Abertawe'r 13eg ganrif fusnes arfordirol llewyrchus gydag ardaloedd fel Gogledd Dyfnaint a phorthladdoedd megis Bryste. Câi nwyddau moethus megis gwin eu mewnforio o Ffrainc ar gyfer trigolion cyfoethog Abertawe'r Oesoedd Canol. Gwnaed gwelliannau i'r afon yn y 19eg ganrif gan greu'r New Cut ym 1843 a symudodd yr afon i'w chwrs presennol.

    Connection with William Cragh

    On an autumn day in the year 1290, William Cragh was hanged on Gibbet Hill by William de Briouze, the Anglo-Norman lord of Gower. Cragh was hanged as a rebel and criminal, together with another Welshman, Trahaearn ap Hywel. In an act of particular cruelty, Lord William forced Cragh’s own friends and family to perform the hanging. Cragh was lifted onto the gibbet first, and then Trahaearn was pulled up by a rope slung over the crossbeam. But one medieval witness at the execution tells us that Trahaearn was ‘big and heavy’, and this caused the gibbet to break. Cragh and Trahaearn were hanged for a second time and seemed to be dead. But was that really the case?

    Medieval Object

    • Floor tile depicting Norman coat of arms, from Neath Abbey (courtesy of Swansea Museum)

       

    With its depiction of the Norman coat of arms of England (three lions passant), this tile represents the power of Anglo-Norman rulers in Wales in the Middle Ages. The lion emblems were introduced to England after the Norman Conquest, and this coat of arms was first used by King Richard I.
