  • Tour information

    By the mid-14th century, medieval Swansea had reached its maximum size and remained like this until the 1800s. This does not mean that the urban landscape stopped changing, however. New buildings were constructed after 1300, such as the town walls and gates, New Castle, and St David’s Hospital. These were all significant building projects, showing that there was money in Swansea at this time to fund these works, as well as a desire by the lord and the townspeople to use them to show off the status of the town to traders and visitors. St David’s Hospital is one of Swansea’s most important medieval landmarks. Now a public house, it is a good place to end the tour of the development of Swansea and reflect on how much the town has changed through the centuries.

    About this place

    Ysbyty Dewi Sant yw un o adeiladau canoloesol pwysicaf Abertawe sy'n sefyll o hyd. Mae tafarn bresennol y Cross Keys yn cynnwys rhannau o'r adeilad o'r 14eg ganrif, sef ysbyty Dewi Fendigaid o Swegnar a sefydlwyd ym 1332. Roedd ysbytai'r Oesoedd Canol yn sefydliadau crefyddol, ac roedd 6 caplan yn gweithio'n Ysbyty Dewi Sant i ddarparu cartref a gofal ar gyfer clerigwyr a lleygwyr hen a methedig. Roedd yr ysbyty mewn lleoliad amlwg a phwysig yn y dref ganoloesol ac mae'r adeiladau sydd wedi goroesi'n dangos bod ganddo ardaloedd preswyl a neuadd gymunedol. Roedd ganddo ardd i dyfu bwyd hefyd. O weddillion y sbwriel a waredwyd yno, gwelodd archaeolegwyr fod gan y preswylwyr fywydau cyfforddus iawn. Roeddent yn mwynhau gwinoedd a nwyddau a fewnforiwyd o Ffrainc, sy'n adlewyrchu cyfoeth a statws yr ysbyty.

    Connection with William Cragh

    Swansea’s South Gate led down towards the Cadle stream and the marshy land beyond. Even here, the raised, imposing structure of the Castle would have been visible, showing the power and control of the Anglo-Norman Marcher lords, who acted quickly to crush and punish acts of rebellion such as the burning of Oystermouth Castle, with which William Cragh was involved.

    Medieval Object

    • 14th-century jug made from local crude ware with green slip / glaze (courtesy of Swansea Museum)

    Between the medieval marketplace on Wind Street and the ferry crossing over the Tawe, the South Gate would have been a place where local people and travellers arrived in the city to enjoy trade and hospitality. This jug must once have been at the centre of many convivial scenes, as people in medieval Swansea shared drinks, stories and laughter.
