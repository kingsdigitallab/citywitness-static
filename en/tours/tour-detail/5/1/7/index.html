Town Walls

  • Tour information

    In 1290, the Welshman William Cragh was captured by William de Briouze, the Anglo-Norman Lord of Gower, for his part in a rebellion and attack on Oystermouth Castle, in Mumbles. The night before his execution, Cragh was held here in the dungeons of Swansea Castle, where he received visions of the Virgin Mary and St Thomas de Cantilupe, a popular saint.

    About this place

    There are references to the dungeon at the castle in 1322, 1400 and 1535 when it was provided with new locks and keys. Despite this where the dungeon was located in Swansea Castle is not known. A later prison for debtors was established in the square tower set in the defensive wall surrounding the castle. In the medieval period, the view from this tower would have looked over the Strand and the River Tawe to the open fields and hills on the opposite bank. In the 18th century the upper floor of the tower was modified to serve as the debtors’ prison, re-establishing the centuries-old link between the Castle and the detention of prisoners involved in rebellions such as William Cragh’s attack on Swansea and Oystermouth Castles in 1287.

    Connection with William Cragh

    Adam of Loughor was a 13-year-old boy in 1290, when the Welsh rebel William Cragh was hanged in Swansea. Interviewed by representatives of the pope years later, Adam remembers that he sat on Swansea’s town walls to get a good view of the hanging. Adam’s story reminds us that executions served as popular spectacle in the Middle Ages – a tense and complex mix of entertainment and reminder of the lord’s power and vengeance. Although Adam sought out the walls as a good vantage-point to watch the hanging, he wasn’t so happy about seeing Cragh’s dead body close up. He tells us that, later, he was too scared to look at Cragh’s bloodied corpse when it lay in the house of Thomas Mathews.

    Medieval Object

    • Medieval cannon ball, found at the site of the Cross Keys Inn (courtesy of Swansea Museum)

    Medieval Swansea was often under siege. This cannon ball reminds us of the many attacks which the town walls had to withstand, and their important role in protecting the town’s inhabitants. Tradition holds that this particular cannon ball was used later, in Oliver Cromwell’s attack on Swansea Castle in the Civil War (1648).
