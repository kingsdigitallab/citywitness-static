St John's Chapel

  • Tour information

    After the hanging, William Cragh’s lifeless body was carried here, the site of the Chapel of St John in medieval Swansea and now St Matthew’s Church. The Chapel may have been chosen as it was outside the town walls and had an historical association with the local Welsh community.

    About this place

    The medieval chapel of St John was located in an area of open fields on the northern outskirts of the walled town of Swansea. Now the site of St Matthew’s church, historical sources indicate that the church was founded by Knights Hospitallers in the 12th century and was associated with a manor they owned. An engraving by Paul Padley from the 1790s shows the earlier church was much smaller, consisting of a simple rectangular building, which would have had a nave and chancel. This engraving also shows that the church was largely surrounded by fields, with a few houses close to the road into Swansea. The present church was built in the 19th century to meet the needs of the growing population of Swansea which saw the fields around the church built over with workers housing.

    Connection with William Cragh

    After William Cragh was hanged, his friends carried his apparently lifeless body to the Chapel of St John. This may have been because the Chapel had a long-standing association with the Welsh community, and it was located outside the town walls. Lady Mary de Briouze tells us that Cragh’s body was transported on a kind of wooden wheel, which in itself would have caused great injury. The different medieval witnesses don’t agree on what happened when Cragh’s body arrived at the Chapel of St John. It seems likely that the Chapel was closed, and Cragh’s body was then carried to the nearby house of Thomas Mathews.

    Medieval Object

    • 13th-century incense burner, found at Penmaen Church (courtesy of Swansea Museum)

    Incense was burned in medieval churches to release perfumed smoke during services. This incense burner or censer is in shaped like an ornate tower, representing the city of God – the heavenly Jerusalem. It reminds us that the heavenly Jerusalem was seen as a model for the earthly city in the Middle Ages – an ideal of perfect peace and harmony, ruled by God, with every member of the community in their proper place.
