St Mary's Church

  • Tour information

    After the creation of the Norman castle town in around 1100, a second phase of development took place to the west, around St Mary’s church. This new area was probably created in the late-12th or 13th centuries, and is a sign that Swansea was prosperous as a town at that time and required more space for its townspeople. The creation of Cross Street, Frog Street, Fisher Street and St Mary’s Street added a new area equivalent at least to Wind Street, doubling the capacity of this part of the town to take in more traders, merchants and craftspeople. The streets form a cross-plan, another piece of medieval town-planning in Swansea. This probably occurred when St Mary’s was built to become the town’s parish church. This new area made the old defences around Wind Street redundant, and a new set of defences were begun to the south of Fisher Street, which were later extended northwards with the building of the town walls.

    About this place

    The church of St Mary was the parish church for medieval Swansea. It was first recorded in 1291 but was probably built originally in the 12th century when this part of the town was first developed. The building has changed much over the centuries, which means little is known about its appearance in the Middle Ages. The church was rebuilt in 1739, although the tower and side chapel of the older church was retained. Elements of the medieval church were recorded and showed evidence of mid-late 13th century architecture prior to its rebuilding between 1895 and 1899. St Mary’s was badly damaged in the Second World War, destroying the remaining medieval fabric. The new church was built between 1954-59, with only the stone effigy of a priest and the 15th century tomb brass to Sir Hugh Johnys and his wife as a link to its medieval past.

    Connection with William Cragh

    The parish church for medieval Swansea, St Mary’s benefited from the patronage of the de Briouze family. We know that Lady Mary de Briouze – wife of the Lord William who ordered the hanging of the Welshman William Cragh – was a religious and devout woman, and we can imagine her visiting St Mary’s for services and prayer. While the medieval structure of St Mary’s doesn’t survive today, the story of William Cragh also gives us other clues about what might have been there. For example, while Cragh is in the dungeons of Swansea Castle before his hanging, he reports seeing a vision of the Virgin Mary. But he says that she wasn’t holding her son, the Christ child, ‘as material images concerning her represent’. It’s possible that Cragh was thinking of pictures of Mary which he had seen in Swansea’s parish church, itself dedicated to her. Wall paintings and statues of Mary were very common in Wales at this time.

    Medieval Object

    • Carved stone head, possibly from the medieval St Mary’s Church (courtesy of Swansea Museum)

    This carved stone head was found at the site of the old Rectory House in Fisher Street, Swansea in the late 19th century and is thought to come from the medieval St Mary’s Church. It is traditionally believed to depict Alina de Mowbray, daughter of Lord William de Briouze (junior) who testified in 1307 as a witness to the hanging of William Cragh. Alina’s own life was colourful and her fortunes very changeable. Today, she is remembered as the ghostly ‘White Lady’ of Oystermouth Castle in Mumbles.
