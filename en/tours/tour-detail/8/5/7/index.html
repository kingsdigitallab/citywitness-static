Town Walls

  • Tour information

    Swansea had town walls by the middle of the 14th century, built of stone, at least for a part of their length, with gates for the streets in and out. The town walls were not built around the whole of the town but just for the part immediately around the castle area, as if the town walls were really a way of creating an outer set of defences for the New Castle that was also being built at this time (to replace the Old Castle). The town walls cut through High Street, and ran behind house-plots in Goat Street, then along the side of St Mary’s Church, to link up with the town ditch that was created to defend the area of Frog Street and Fisher Street to the south. The town gates have all gone, but from archaeological research and by studying the town’s street pattern it is possible to work out where the gates were, as well as the circuit of the walls.

    About this place

    Medieval Swansea was a walled town. The line of walls is in part recalled by the name Whitewalls and ran on the western (inland) side of the town connecting West Gate and Wassail Gate. The walls here were made of stone. Historical documents refer to a tax in 1317 for the upkeep of the walls while archaeology has shown that the stone defences may be contemporary with the rebuilding of the castle in the late 13th century. At the corner of Whitewalls and St Mary’s Square, the wall was shown to be 1.9 metres thick, faced with sandstone blocks. In front of the wall was a ditch about 6 metres wide and 2 metres deep. The line of the defences around the town can be followed by walking along the street still called Whitewalls.

    Connection with William Cragh

    Adam of Loughor was a 13-year-old boy in 1290, when the Welsh rebel William Cragh was hanged in Swansea. Interviewed by representatives of the pope years later, Adam remembers that he sat on Swansea’s town walls to get a good view of the hanging. Adam’s story reminds us that executions served as popular spectacle in the Middle Ages – a tense and complex mix of entertainment and reminder of the lord’s power and vengeance. Although Adam sought out the walls as a good vantage-point to watch the hanging, he wasn’t so happy about seeing Cragh’s dead body close up. He tells us that, later, he was too scared to look at Cragh’s bloodied corpse when it lay in the house of Thomas Mathews.

    Medieval Object

    • Medieval cannon ball, found at the site of the Cross Keys Inn (courtesy of Swansea Museum)

    Medieval Swansea was often under siege. This cannon ball reminds us of the many attacks which the town walls had to withstand, and their important role in protecting the town’s inhabitants. Tradition holds that this particular cannon ball was used later, in Oliver Cromwell’s attack on Swansea Castle in the Civil War (1648).
