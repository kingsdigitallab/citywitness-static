  • Tour information

    William Cragh, the Welshman hanged by Lord William de Briouze in 1290, spent the night before his execution here with other condemned men. The castle dungeons were a place of suffering and despair, where criminals and opponents of the Anglo-Norman regime were held. Later, in the 18th century, part of Swansea Castle became a debtors’ prison. Is this place still haunted by the pleas and moans of the unfortunate individuals who languished here?

    About this place

    There are references to the dungeon at the castle in 1322, 1400 and 1535 when it was provided with new locks and keys. Despite this where the dungeon was located in Swansea Castle is not known. A later prison for debtors was established in the square tower set in the defensive wall surrounding the castle. In the medieval period, the view from this tower would have looked over the Strand and the River Tawe to the open fields and hills on the opposite bank. In the 18th century the upper floor of the tower was modified to serve as the debtors’ prison, re-establishing the centuries-old link between the Castle and the detention of prisoners involved in rebellions such as William Cragh’s attack on Swansea and Oystermouth Castles in 1287.

    Connection with William Cragh

    On an autumn day in the year 1290, William Cragh was hanged on Gibbet Hill by William de Briouze, the Anglo-Norman lord of Gower. Cragh was hanged as a rebel and criminal, together with another Welshman, Trahaearn ap Hywel. In an act of particular cruelty, Lord William forced Cragh’s own friends and family to perform the hanging. Cragh was lifted onto the gibbet first, and then Trahaearn was pulled up by a rope slung over the crossbeam. But one medieval witness at the execution tells us that Trahaearn was ‘big and heavy’, and this caused the gibbet to break. Cragh and Trahaearn were hanged for a second time and seemed to be dead. But was that really the case?
