The Dungeon

  • Tour information

    The role of Mary de Briouze, Lady of Swansea Castle, in the hanging of the Welshman William Cragh, is strange and mysterious. Mary’s husband, Lord William de Briouze, ordered the execution of Cragh. But Mary begged for Cragh’s life to be spared. When her husband refused, she then prayed to St Thomas de Cantilupe, a popular saint, to intervene. As Cragh began to recover, she sent her maidservants – and possibly her own daughter – to nurse him back to health. Why did Lady Mary care so much about a condemned Welsh outlaw? Could there be a story of secret allegiances or even forbidden love hidden here…?

    About this place

    New Castle was designed to replace the Old Castle, and work on it started around 1284-90 under the lordship of William de Briouze II. The distinctive arcading seen high up on the standing walls was added by John, Lord of Mowbray (1331-54). Swansea’s New Castle reflects a period of relative peace with few raids or wars. Being designed to provide comfortable living and working space, the New Castle had well-appointed private rooms with an inside toilet (latrine) that emptied into the moat. There was also a grand entertainment and business area – a hall – located on the first floor with a series of basements below. By this time there is also evidence that housing for wealthier residents of Swansea stood in the outer ward of the castle. This area was cleared in the 1960s and 1970s to expose the ruins of the castle and to create Castle Square.

    Connection with William Cragh

    William Cragh was held in the dungeons of Swansea’s New Castle before he was led to Gibbet Hill to be hanged. While in the dungeons, he tells us that he had a vision of a man robed in white, whom he believed to be St Thomas de Cantilupe. He also saw a vision of the Virgin Mary, who gave him a sign that he would be saved. While in the dungeon, Cragh ‘bent a penny to St Thomas’ – a way of asking for the saint’s help and promising to honour him if he was spared.

    Medieval Object

    • Silver coin from the reign of Edward I (1272-1307), found in Upper Killay (courtesy of Swansea Museum)

    Waiting in the dungeons, the night before his execution, William Cragh tells us that he ‘bent a penny’ to St Thomas. This folk custom involved bending or folding a coin to invoke a saint and their help. This coin, from the reign of Edward I, recalls the secret ritual practised by Cragh – which may perhaps have saved his life…
